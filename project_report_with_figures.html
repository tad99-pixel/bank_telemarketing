Introduction

Banks spend significant resources on telephone marketing campaigns to promote financial products such as term deposits. A key challenge is identifying which clients are likely to subscribe, so that call-centre efforts can be focused on high-probability leads. Mis-targeting not only wastes resources but also risks alienating customers. Accurate predictive models therefore have direct commercial and operational value. The task is framed as a binary classification problem: given client demographics, prior contact history, and macroeconomic indicators, predict whether a client will subscribe to a term deposit (yes/no).

Moro, Cortez, and Rita (2014) demonstrated that semi-automatic feature selection combined with Data Mining (DM) models can achieve ROC-AUC above 0.80. Their CRISP-DM framework highlighted the importance of careful preprocessing but did not explore customer segmentation prior to classification. Farquad, Vadlamani, and Surampudi (2014) showed that balancing class distributions substantially improves recall for the minority (subscribed) class. Elsalamony (2013) found that model performance could be improved when heterogeneous sub-populations exist in the data. These findings motivate our cluster-then-classify strategy, which first partitions customers into coherent groups and then fits a dedicated model per group.

Our principal contribution is a two-stage pipeline that addresses two limitations of prior work: (i) the heterogeneity problem, solved by K-Means segmentation, and (ii) severe class imbalance, addressed by custom decision-threshold tuning. Each cluster’s logistic regression model can also be individually inspected, improving interpretability.

Dataset

We use the Bank Marketing dataset (Moro, Cortez, and Rita 2014) containing 41,188 instances and 20 features collected from a Portuguese bank’s direct marketing campaigns from May 2008 to November 2010. The target variable y is highly imbalanced: approximately 88.7% of records are labelled no, and 11.3% are labelled yes, reflecting the natural scarcity of successful subscriptions.

Features. The feature set spans four categories: (a) client attributes — age, job, marital status, education, credit default, housing loan, personal loan; (b) last contact information — contact type, month, day-of-week; (c) campaign statistics — number of contacts, days since prior contact (pdays), prior outcomes; and (d) socioeconomic indicators — employment variation rate, consumer price index, consumer confidence index, EURIBOR 3-month rate, and number of employees.

Preprocessing. The duration column is dropped to prevent data leakage. Categorical placeholder values encoded as unknown or with a value of -1 in six columns — job, marital, education, default, housing, and loan — are imputed with the column mode inside a sklearn pipeline so that no test-set information contaminates the imputation. A 90/10 train–test split is applied (random state 1244).

EDA highlights. Students and retired clients show subscription rates above 20%, roughly double the overall rate. Success rate peaks at one or two calls and drops sharply beyond five contacts, motivating an optimal-frequency constraint. Previously contacted customers (pdays ≠ 999) exhibit a 65% higher success rate than first-time contacts.

Methods

This work tests the following models, starting with the Single Logistic Regression (LR) model as a baseline without clustering. This is followed by a comparison with a Single Logistic Regression model with class weight balancing and our proposed Cluster-LR model, which addresses data heterogeneity via K-Means segmentation, motivated by Elsalamony (2014). All models are tested on a held-out 10% test set, with K-Means tuning and per-cluster Logistic Regression subject to 5-fold cross-validation.

We selected the F1 score as the primary performance metric, given its suitability for highly imbalanced datasets and the balanced prioritization of minimizing both wasted resources (false positives) and missed revenue opportunities (false negatives).

Our pipeline consists of three stages executed within a single scikit-learn ImbPipeline: (1) missing-value imputation, (2) unsupervised customer segmentation via K-Means, and (3) supervised per-cluster Logistic Regression.

The pipeline handles preprocessing within training folds to prevent leakage throughout. duration is dropped at the outset, followed by mode imputation of missing values across six columns. For clustering, one-hot encoding expands the feature space to 50 dimensions; PCA then reduces this to components capturing 80% of variance before K-Means is applied, mitigating the curse of dimensionality. The optimal number of clusters is selected objectively via silhouette score rather than the subjective elbow method. Finally, a dedicated Logistic Regression (ElasticNet regularisation) is trained per cluster, with hyperparameters tuned by 5-fold CV on ROC-AUC. Class imbalance is addressed through per-cluster decision threshold tuning — sweeping 0.1–0.9 to find the threshold that maximises F1 — rather than relying on the default 0.5 cutoff which would be biased toward the majority class.

Results and Discussion

On clustering. GridSearchCV identified k = 2 clusters as optimal (silhouette score ≈ 0.142). Subscription rates range from approximately 5% in Cluster 0 to approximately 25% in Cluster 1, confirming that segmentation reveals genuine sub-populations.

One difference between these two groups of customers is the economic environment they were in when the bank called them. Cluster 0 is defined by a period of high interest rates and high employment levels. While high interest rates generally encourage term deposits, the report suggests there were other priorities that made customers hesitant to lock their money away. A plausible explanation is that due to the positive economic outlook and steady employment levels, customers had a larger appetite for risk and were less receptive to more conservative financial products like term deposits. Besides the economic environment, the two groups can also be differentiated by how much brand loyalty they have with the bank. Cluster 1 represents customers who have had several successful interactions with the bank in the past. Cluster 0, on the other hand, consists of customers who have little to no history with the bank.

Classification performance. To manage the severe class imbalance and to facilitate the bank’s objective of maximising revenue while minimising cost, a multi-metric evaluation approach was employed. The Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC-ROC) was used as a threshold-independent measure to show that the model performed better than the baseline as a preliminary metric. For a targeted marketing strategy, the F1 score was prioritized as the main metric.

Table 2 reports performance on the held-out test set (10%, n ≈ 4,119). The proposed cluster-aware model achieves ROC-AUC of 0.793, outperforming the single-LR baseline (0.769). Per-cluster threshold tuning boosts minority-class F1 from 0.42 to 0.51, the primary metric given class imbalance.

The F1 score was chosen as it balances precision and recall, which directly translates to business value. Precision quantifies the rate of true positives among all positive predictions, minimizing the cost of a false positive (a wasted call-center resource). Recall quantifies the rate of true positives among all actual subscriptions, minimizing the cost of a false negative (a missed revenue opportunity). While the baseline model has higher precision, its low recall means missing two-thirds of potential subscribers. The Cluster-LR model accepts slightly lower precision to achieve materially higher recall, significantly reducing missed revenue opportunities.

Human comparison. Experienced telemarketers achieve roughly 50–60% precision for subscription prediction based on brief call profiling. The model’s precision of 52% is competitive without any real-time interaction, and it scales to tens of thousands of prospects simultaneously, making it useful in practice even without surpassing human accuracy.

Societal impact. Privacy: the model uses only CRM and macroeconomic data, with no sensitive data beyond standard banking records. Fairness: cluster membership could inadvertently encode protected attributes such as age or marital status, so future work should audit cluster-level disparate impact. Jobs: automation of lead scoring may reduce call-centre headcount but frees agents for more personal interactions with customers.
